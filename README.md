## 📌 Project Overview

Stroke remains one of the leading causes of death and long-term disability worldwide, yet many of its risk factors are measurable, visible, and preventable long before an event occurs.

This dashboard analyses health and lifestyle data from **4,909 individuals** to uncover which factors most strongly correlate with stroke occurrence  including hypertension, glucose levels, BMI, smoking status, heart disease, age, and gender. The goal is to move beyond a single risk number and build a clear, multi-factor picture of stroke risk that can support early screening and preventive healthcare decisions.

## 📊 Dashboard Features

- **Top KPI Cards** — Total Persons, Stroke Cases, Stroke Rate, Average BMI, and Average Glucose Level
- **Stroke Distribution** — donut chart showing the overall split between stroke and non-stroke cases
- **Average BMI by Stroke** — bar chart comparing BMI between individuals who had a stroke versus those who did not
- **Average Glucose Level by Stroke** — bar chart comparing blood glucose levels across stroke and non-stroke groups
- **Stroke Rate by Smoking Status** — horizontal bar chart breaking stroke rate down by smoking history (formerly smoked, smokes, never smoked, unknown)
- **Stroke Rate by Hypertension** — bar chart comparing stroke rate between individuals with and without hypertension
- **Stroke Rate by Age** — horizontal bar chart segmenting stroke rate across age cohorts (children/adolescents, young adults, middle-aged, older adults)
- **Stroke by Heart Disease** — bar chart comparing stroke rate between individuals with and without heart disease
- **Stroke by Gender** — bar chart comparing stroke rate between male and female individuals
- **Interactive Filters** — Ever Married, Work Type, Residence Type, and Gender, allowing dynamic exploration of the dataset
- **Key Insights Panel** — a built-in summary box highlighting the dashboard's most important findings directly within the report

## 🔍 Key Insights

1. **Hypertension is the strongest single risk factor identified** — individuals with hypertension experienced a stroke rate of **13.30%**, nearly four times higher than the **3.34%** rate seen in those without hypertension.

2. **Heart disease dramatically increases stroke risk** — stroke rate among individuals with heart disease was **16.46%**, compared to just **3.62%** for those without — making heart disease one of the most significant predictors in this dataset.

3. **Age is a major driver of stroke risk** — older adults recorded a stroke rate of **11.67%**, far exceeding middle-aged individuals (**3.51%**), young adults (**0.43%**), and children/adolescents (**0.11%**). Risk increases sharply and consistently with age.

4. **Smoking history correlates strongly with stroke occurrence** — individuals who **formerly smoked** had the highest stroke rate at **6.81%**, even higher than current smokers (**5.29%**), suggesting that cumulative smoking history matters as much as current smoking status. Those who never smoked had the lowest rate at **4.54%**.

5. **Elevated glucose levels are clearly linked to stroke** — individuals who had a stroke recorded an average glucose level of **135**, compared to **104** among those who did not — a gap wide enough to make glucose level a meaningful early warning indicator.

6. **BMI is moderately elevated among stroke patients** — average BMI among stroke cases was **30** versus **29** for non-stroke cases. While the gap is smaller than other factors, it still points in the same direction as the rest of the data.

7. **Gender shows only a slight difference in stroke occurrence** — males recorded a stroke rate of **4.43%** compared to **4.14%** in females, indicating gender is a much weaker predictor relative to hypertension, heart disease, or age.

8. **The overall population stroke rate is 4.26%** — meaning roughly 1 in every 24 people in this dataset experienced a stroke, reinforcing why early identification of high-risk individuals (via hypertension, heart disease, and age) is critical for preventive care.

## 💡 Recommendations

- **Prioritise hypertension screening and management** — given it nearly quadruples stroke risk, hypertension should be treated as a primary red flag in any preventive screening programme
- **Treat heart disease patients as a high-priority monitoring group** — with a stroke rate nearly 5x higher than those without heart disease, this population warrants closer, more frequent clinical follow-up
- **Implement age-tiered screening protocols** — older adults should be screened for stroke risk factors far more proactively and frequently than younger cohorts, given the sharp rise in risk with age
- **Don't overlook former smokers** — since former smokers showed a higher stroke rate than current smokers, screening protocols should not assume risk drops to baseline simply because someone has quit
- **Incorporate glucose monitoring into routine stroke risk assessments** — the clear gap between stroke and non-stroke glucose averages suggests this is a useful, low-cost early indicator worth tracking consistently
